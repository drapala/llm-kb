## Introduction

The mass media function to communicate messages and inculcate individuals with values that integrate them into institutional structures. In societies with concentrated wealth and class conflicts, fulfilling this role "requires systematic propaganda."

The propaganda model is less visible in systems with private media and absent formal censorship, yet operates through structural inequalities in resources and access.

---

## The Five Filters of the Propaganda Model

### Filter 1 — Size, Ownership, and Profit Orientation

Media ownership has become increasingly concentrated. Despite over 25,000 media entities in the U.S., the largest companies dominate news distribution. "The twenty-nine largest media systems account for over half of the output."

Capital requirements for establishing viable news operations have grown dramatically. Nineteenth-century newspapers required modest investment; by the twentieth century, startup costs reached millions of dollars, effectively excluding smaller operations or alternative voices.

The top tier comprises 10–24 systems that define the news agenda. These companies are large, profit-seeking corporations owned by wealthy individuals, with median assets exceeding $2.6 billion.

**Integration with Power:** Media boards interlock with corporate and banking executives. These companies depend on government licenses and have symbiotic relationships with political authority.

### Filter 2 — Advertising as Primary Income Source

Advertising functions as a "de facto licensing authority" for media viability. Papers lacking advertising revenue face higher costs and reduced market share, eventually failing or becoming marginalized.

The advertising system structurally disadvantages working-class and radical media. Advertisers target affluent audiences with purchasing power, not mass audiences generally. As one executive stated in 1856, journals with poor readers are "vehicles" where "money thrown upon them is so much thrown away."

**Historical Example:** The Daily Herald, with 4.7 million readers, received only 3.5% of national advertising revenue and eventually failed despite superior readership satisfaction.

Modern networks openly acknowledge targeting audience "quality" for advertiser optimization rather than serving democratic interests.

### Filter 3 — Reliance on Official Sources

The media depend on government, corporate, and approved expert sources due to economic necessity and news production demands. The Pentagon's public information operation dwarfs independent voices — employing thousands and spending hundreds of millions annually.

Mark Fishman's "principle of bureaucratic affinity" explains why news organizations rely on official sources: "only other bureaucracies can satisfy the input needs of a news bureaucracy."

Official sources gain "routine" status and privileged access. Critical sources face higher verification costs and risk offending powerful information providers.

**Expert Co-optation:** The government and corporations have deliberately funded think tanks and hired intellectuals to shape expert discourse. Judge Lewis Powell urged business to "buy the top academic reputations in the country" to influence public debate.

### Filter 4 — Flak as a Disciplinary Mechanism

"Flak" refers to organized negative responses — letters, lawsuits, boycotts, threats. Large-scale flak from powerful actors imposes significant costs on media organizations.

The corporate community has sponsored institutions specifically designed to produce flak: the American Legal Foundation, Media Institute, Accuracy in Media (AIM), and others. These organizations attack media coverage deemed insufficiently pro-business or hawkish on foreign policy.

AIM's budget grew from $5,000 in 1971 to $1.5 million by the early 1980s, funded by large corporations. Freedom House, founded in the 1940s, has served as "a virtual propaganda arm of the government and international right wing."

The threat of flak conditions media behavior even preventively, discouraging coverage likely to trigger powerful opposition.

### Filter 5 — Anticommunism as Control Mechanism (historical; updated to anti-ideology)

Anticommunism functions as an ideological filter that fragments opposition movements and justifies support for fascist regimes as "lesser evils."

This ideology elevates opposition to communism to a "first principle of Western ideology." When anticommunist fervor rises, "the demand for serious evidence in support of claims of 'communist' abuses is suspended," allowing unreliable sources to gain credibility.

**Political Effects:** The ideology pressures even liberals to behave like reactionaries. It has justified CIA coups (Guatemala), military intervention (Dominican Republic), and support for authoritarian regimes that suppress unions and dissent.

## How Filters Interact: Dichotomization and Propaganda Campaigns

The five filters operate synergistically to narrow acceptable news. Stories from establishment sources easily pass through; dissidents face sourcing disadvantages, credibility questions, and organized opposition.

### Worthy vs. Unworthy Victims

The model predicts systematic dichotomization based on utility to elite interests:

- **Turkish repression (1980):** U.S.-aligned government; covered minimally despite systematic abuses
- **Polish dissidents (1981):** Soviet-aligned government; extensively covered, framed as noble cause

The New York Times explained this difference explicitly: regarding Israel's 1973 shooting down of a Libyan civilian airliner, "No useful purpose is served by an acrimonious debate."

### Propaganda Campaign Mechanics

Campaigns begin with government leaks, press conferences, or major media articles. Once coverage initiates, other outlets follow, creating familiarity that establishes "truth" without evidence.

**Examples:**
- The "Bulgarian Connection" and pope assassination plot (Reader's Digest led campaign)
- KAL 007 Soviet downing (Reagan administration arms advocacy)
- Cambodian atrocities (useful for Cold War narratives; U.S. bombing of Cambodia ignored)

Dissenting views face flak and become incompatible with "established" narratives. Media abandon campaigns quietly when they collapse under fabrication weight.

### Predictable Coverage Patterns

The model anticipates:

- Official sources used uncritically for friendly governments
- Refugee/dissident sources emphasized for enemy states
- Different evaluation standards applied to allies vs. adversaries
- Worthy victims: prominent placement, humanization, detailed context
- Unworthy victims: minimal coverage, dehumanization, little contextual detail
- Sharp restrictions on opinion column range

---

## Conclusion

The propaganda model explains "systematic and highly political dichotomization in news coverage based on serviceability to important domestic power interests." These patterns are "massive and systematic," observable across story selection, volume, placement, tone, and treatment depth.

The system operates "naturally" through structural constraints rather than explicit censorship, allowing journalists to maintain genuine belief in objectivity while functioning within filters "built into the system in such a fundamental way, that alternative bases of news choices are hardly imaginable."
