## Overview

C. Wright Mills' seminal 1956 work examines how power concentrates among political, economic, and military leaders in America. Mills argues that institutional structures have created unprecedented coordination among these three domains — what he calls the "power elite."

## Five Historical Epochs of American Power

Mills identifies five distinct periods in American power structure development:

1. **Revolutionary era:** Unified elites easily moved between political, economic, and military roles
2. **Early 19th century:** Looser coalition of diverse top groups with no centralized control
3. **Post-Civil War to WWI:** Corporate economic dominance; government subordinate to business interests
4. **New Deal era:** Political power contested among business, labor, and farm groups
5. **Post-WWII:** Military-corporate coordination in "permanent war economy"

## The Current Power Elite

Three structural trends define the modern (post-WWII) era:

1. The professional politician has been "relegated to middle levels of power" while corporate executives and military officers ascend to top decision-making positions
2. Military needs and corporate interests increasingly coincide, strengthening both institutions while weakening democratic oversight
3. The three domains — political, economic, military — have come to interpenetrate and coordinate at the top, creating a unified elite that did not exist in earlier periods

## Common Characteristics and Social Homogeneity

Elite members share similar origins: predominantly Protestant, educated at Ivy League schools, from upper-class backgrounds, native-born Americans. This homogeneity creates psychological affinities through clubs, boards, and social networks that cement cooperation across institutional boundaries.

## Personnel Interchange — The Inner Core

The "inner core" consists of individuals who move between military, corporate, and political positions throughout their careers. Investment bankers and corporate lawyers serve as key "go-betweens" linking these domains.

This **circulation of elites** means that the power elite is not a fixed caste but a fluid network with permeable but real boundaries.

## Not a Conspiracy

Mills explicitly addresses the conspiracy objection: the theory does not require proving secret plots or coordinated conspiracy. Rather, structural incentives naturally produce cooperation among elites sharing common interests, backgrounds, and institutional positions.

"These men operate within codes established by their circles — they cannot simply transcend their institutional conditioning."

## Public vs. Mass Society (Democratic Decline)

Mills introduces a key distinction:
- **Public:** citizens express opinions and effectively challenge authority
- **Mass:** individuals passively receive communications with minimal capacity to respond or organize counter-action

The transformation from "public" to "mass" society represents a crucial consequence of elite consolidation. As top institutions grow more powerful and unified, middle-level balances weaken, and mass publics lose meaningful political agency.

## The Theory of Balance (Ch. 11)

Mills critiques the pluralist theory of democratic balance of power. He argues that:
- The "balance of power" described by liberals refers to a historical period (New Deal era) that has passed
- Middle levels of power (Congress, interest groups) still exist but no longer determine the most consequential decisions
- Top decisions — war, economic policy, technology — are made at the level of the power elite, insulated from middle-level pressures

## The Power Elite (Ch. 12)

The power elite is not identical to a "ruling class" in the Marxist sense:
- It is not a cohesive ideological bloc but a practical coordination network
- Its unity is situational and institutional, not conspiratorial
- Its power operates "behind the scenes" through institutional position, not merely through legal authority or ownership

Mills distinguishes his concept from both:
1. Marxist "ruling class" (too focused on economic determination)
2. Pluralist "no dominant group" (empirically false at the top level)
