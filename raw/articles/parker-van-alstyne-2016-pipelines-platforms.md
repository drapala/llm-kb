# Pipelines, Platforms, and the New Rules of Strategy

**Authors:** Marshall W. Van Alstyne, Geoffrey G. Parker, Sangeet Paul Choudary
**Source:** Harvard Business Review, April 2016
**URL:** https://hbr.org/2016/04/pipelines-platforms-and-the-new-rules-of-strategy

---

## Idea in Brief

**The Sea Change:** Platform businesses that bring together producers and consumers (Uber, Airbnb) are gobbling up market share and transforming competition. Traditional businesses that fail to create platforms and learn the new rules of strategy will struggle.

**The New Rules:** With a platform, the critical asset is the community and the resources of its members. The focus of strategy shifts from controlling to orchestrating resources, from optimizing internal processes to facilitating external interactions, and from increasing customer value to maximizing ecosystem value.

**The Upshot:** Competition can emerge from seemingly unrelated industries or from within the platform itself. Firms must make smart choices about whom to let onto platforms and what they're allowed to do there, and must track new metrics designed to monitor and boost platform interactions.

## Main Article

Back in 2007 the five major mobile-phone manufacturers—Nokia, Samsung, Motorola, Sony Ericsson, and LG—collectively controlled 90% of the industry's global profits. That year, Apple's iPhone burst onto the scene. By 2015 the iPhone singlehandedly generated 92% of global profits, while all but one of the former incumbents made no profit at all.

Apple (along with Google's Android) overran the incumbents by exploiting the power of platforms. Platform businesses bring together producers and consumers in high-value exchanges. Their chief assets are information and interactions. Apple conceived the iPhone as a way to connect participants in two-sided markets—app developers on one side and app users on the other—generating value for both groups. By January 2015 the App Store offered 1.4 million apps and had cumulatively generated $25 billion for developers.

### Pipeline to Platform

Platforms have existed for years—malls link consumers and merchants; newspapers connect subscribers and advertisers. What's changed is that IT has profoundly reduced the need to own physical infrastructure. IT makes building and scaling up platforms simpler and cheaper, allows nearly frictionless participation that strengthens network effects, and enhances the ability to capture, analyze, and exchange data.

All platforms have an ecosystem with the same basic structure comprising four types of players:
- **Owners** control intellectual property and governance
- **Providers** serve as the platform's interface with users
- **Producers** create offerings
- **Consumers** use those offerings

Apple's handset business is essentially a pipeline. Combine it with the App Store and you have a platform. Firms can be both. When platforms enter the same marketplace as pipeline businesses, the platforms virtually always win.

### The Three Key Shifts

**1. From resource control to resource orchestration.** In a pipeline world, firms gain advantage by controlling scarce and valuable assets—mines, real estate, IP. With platforms, the assets that are hard to copy are the community and the resources its members own and contribute (rooms, cars, ideas, information).

**2. From internal optimization to external interaction.** Pipeline firms optimize an entire chain of product activities. Platforms create value by facilitating interactions between external producers and consumers. They shed variable costs of production. The emphasis shifts from dictating processes to persuading participants.

**3. From a focus on customer value to a focus on ecosystem value.** Pipelines seek to maximize the lifetime value of individual customers. Platforms seek to maximize the total value of an expanding ecosystem in a circular, iterative, feedback-driven process. Sometimes subsidizing one type of consumer attracts another.

### The Power of Network Effects

The engine of the industrial economy was supply-side economies of scale: massive fixed costs and low marginal costs mean firms achieving higher volume have lower average costs—a virtuous feedback loop that produces monopolies. This gave us Carnegie Steel, Standard Oil, GE.

The driving force behind the internet economy is demand-side economies of scale, also known as network effects. In the internet economy, firms that achieve higher "volume" (more platform participants) offer higher average value per transaction, because the larger the network, the better the matches between supply and demand. Greater scale → more value → more participants → more value. This produced Alibaba (75% of Chinese e-commerce), Google (82% of mobile OS, 94% of mobile search), and Facebook.

The five forces model doesn't factor in network effects. It regards external forces as "depletive." In demand-side economies, external forces can be "accretive"—adding value to the platform.

### How Platforms Change Strategy

**Forces within the ecosystem.** Platform participants can defect or compete directly. Zynga began as a games producer on Facebook but sought to migrate players to its own platform. Amazon and Samsung, Android providers, tried to create their own OS versions. Netflix, a provider on telecom platforms, can extract value while relying on their infrastructure.

Consumers and producers can swap roles: Uber riders become drivers; Airbnb travelers become hosts.

**Networks Invert the Firm.** Companies are moving toward orchestrating external networks:
- Marketing: consumers propagate messages (Warby Parker optical)
- IT: supports external communities (Threadless T-shirts)
- HR: leverages external talent (SAP opening internal developer network)
- Finance: distributed ledgers shared externally (IBM, Intel, JPMorgan blockchain)
- Operations: "not-even-mine" inventory (Airbnb, Uber, YouTube)

**Forces exerted by ecosystems.** Platform competition can emerge from seemingly unrelated industries. Swatch must now compete with Apple; Siemens with Google's Nest. Three competitive threat patterns:
1. Established platform with superior network effects enters your industry
2. Competitor targets overlapping customers with new platform (Airbnb vs hotels)
3. Data collector enters your market (health care: traditional providers vs Fitbit vs Walgreens)

### Access and Governance

**Harnessing Spillovers.** Book purchases generate recommendations. Uber rides generate driver ratings that improve platform value for others. Data on ride quality enables optimal pricing across the platform.

**Architecture and Governance:** Platforms must decide how open both should be. An open architecture lets players create new value; open governance lets others shape rules and rewards.

- Open architecture, no reward sharing: ability but no incentive
- Open governance, closed architecture: incentive but no ability
- **Permissionless innovation:** Rovio created Angry Birds on Apple without approval, with confidence Apple wouldn't steal its IP

However, unfettered access destroys value through "noise": Chatroulette's "naked hairy man" problem caused collapse of initially-open platform.

Most successful platforms manage openness carefully: Airbnb and Uber rate/insure participants; Twitter/Facebook provide anti-stalking tools; App Store and Google Play filter low-quality apps.

### Metrics

New metrics platform managers must track:

**Interaction failure:** If Lyft shows "no cars available," the platform failed to match supply and demand. Failures reduce network effects: riders quit → higher driver downtime → drivers quit → lower availability (negative feedback loop).

**Engagement:** Healthy platforms track participation that enhances network effects—content sharing, repeat visits. Facebook watches ratio of daily to monthly users.

**Match quality:** Google constantly monitors clicking and reading to refine search results.

**Negative network effects:** Congestion caused by unconstrained network growth can discourage participation. Misbehavior creates negative loops.

In 2016, Uber's private equity valuation exceeded GM's despite being founded in 2009 vs GM's 1908 founding—investors were looking beyond traditional financials.

### New Leadership for a Platform World

Rupert Murdoch bought Myspace and managed it top-down like a newspaper, focusing on controlling the internal operation rather than fostering the ecosystem. The community dissipated and the platform withered.

The skills for tightly controlling internal resources don't apply to nurturing external ecosystems. Traditional pipeline firms must develop new core competencies and a new mindset to design, govern, and nimbly expand platforms.
