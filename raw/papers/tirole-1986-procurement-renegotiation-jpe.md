# Procurement and Renegotiation

**Author:** Jean Tirole
**Source:** Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 94, No. 2 (April 1986), pp. 235-259
**DOI:** 10.1086/261372
**Working paper:** MIT Department of Economics WP #362 (1985)

## Abstract (reconstructed from secondary sources)

This paper analyzes procurement contracts in a setting where parties bound by an incomplete contract have an incentive to renegotiate after acquiring new information. In a two-period procurement model, a buyer and a seller sign an initial contract; subsequently, both acquire private information about costs and valuations. The paper shows that the vendor generally underinvests in the first period because it anticipates that the buyer will attempt to capture the value generated by investment through renegotiation (the "hold-up" problem). Optimal contracts combine fixed-price and cost-plus elements to balance incentives and risk-sharing.

## Key Results

**Hold-up problem:** Vendor invests optimally only if it can capture sufficient share of created value. When the buyer can renegotiate downward, the vendor captures fraction α < 1 of investment returns → underinvestment equilibrium.

**Contract types:**
- Fixed-price contracts: vendor bears all cost risk → strong incentives, but buyer exposed to cost overruns
- Cost-plus contracts: buyer bears all cost risk → no incentive to reduce costs (moral hazard)
- Optimal contract: mixture that partially exposes vendor to cost risk

**Renegotiation-proof contracts:** Generally infeasible in procurement due to unforeseeable contingencies, evolving scope, and ex-post information acquisition.

**Incomplete contracts:** The paper assumes parties cannot write contingent contracts for all states of nature — fundamental to the incomplete contracts literature (contemporaneous with Grossman & Hart 1986).

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

- **Additive contracts (aditivos) as hold-up instrument:** Brazilian law (Lei 8.666/93) permits contracts to grow by up to 25% without new bidding. This is precisely the renegotiation channel Tirole models. Vendors who win with low bids and then request aditivos may be executing a strategic hold-up.
- **Signal:** Frequency of contracts reaching the 25% additive limit, or aditivos requested in final project phases, is a proxy for strategic renegotiation behavior.
- **Prior work:** Williamson (1975) Markets and Hierarchies describes hold-up informally. Tirole (1986) is among the first formal procurement models.
