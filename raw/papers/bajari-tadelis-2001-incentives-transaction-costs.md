# Incentives versus Transaction Costs: A Theory of Procurement Contracts

**Authors:** Patrick Bajari and Steven Tadelis
**Source:** RAND Journal of Economics, Vol. 32, No. 3 (Autumn 2001), pp. 387-407
**DOI:** 10.2307/2696361
**NBER Working Paper:** w6881

## Abstract

We develop a theory of procurement contract choice that explains when buyers use fixed-price (FP) contracts versus cost-plus (C+) contracts. In our model, FP contracts provide stronger incentives for cost reduction but impose larger renegotiation costs when design changes are needed. C+ contracts have weaker incentives but lower adaptation costs. The theory predicts FP contracts for simple, well-specified projects and C+ contracts for complex projects where scope is uncertain. We find empirical support using data from building construction.

## Key Results

**Trade-off framework:**
- **Fixed-price (FP):** Contractor bears full cost risk → maximum incentive for cost reduction and efficiency. But: if scope changes, renegotiation is costly (each change requires new price negotiation). High renegotiation transaction costs.
- **Cost-plus (C+):** Buyer bears full cost risk → weak cost-reduction incentives (contractor has no skin in the game). But: scope changes are cheap to accommodate (just reimburse costs). Low adaptation costs.

**Optimal contract choice:**
- Simple, well-specified projects (low design uncertainty) → FP contracts (strong incentives matter more; few changes expected)
- Complex, uncertain projects (high design uncertainty, frequent scope changes) → C+ contracts (adaptation costs dominate)

**Empirical validation:** Using building construction data — residential (simple) vs commercial (complex). Residential: more FP. Commercial/industrial: more C+. Pattern consistent with theory.

**Renegotiation frequency:** FP contracts lead to more renegotiation (design changes are costly to adapt) but each renegotiation is explicit and costly. C+ contracts lead to smoother adaptation but weaker incentives.

## Why Aditivos Happen: The Bajari-Tadelis Lens

In Brazilian public procurement (Lei 8.666/93), *all* contracts must be bid competitively using a price-based selection — essentially forcing FP-like contracts even for complex projects. But complex infrastructure projects have high design uncertainty → high expected renegotiation frequency.

The result: government is forced to use FP-style contracts for complex projects → high renegotiation costs → aditivos as the mechanism to accommodate scope changes.

**Two types of aditivos through this lens:**
1. *Efficient aditivos:* genuine scope changes on a complex project that the law forces to be FP. These are unavoidable under the current procurement regime.
2. *Strategic aditivos:* contractors deliberately underbid knowing they'll request aditivos — exploiting the legal 25% threshold as a tool.

**Distinguishing signal for Zelox:** Efficient aditivos should correlate with: project complexity, specific types of scope changes (measured), and early detection in project lifecycle. Strategic aditivos correlate with: consistently reaching exactly 25% threshold, late-project requests, pattern across multiple contracts by same vendor.

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

**Explains structural origin of aditivos:** Even without corruption, Brazilian procurement law creates conditions where aditivos are economically rational. This means:
1. Not all aditivos are signals of corruption
2. The *pattern* of aditivos (timing, magnitude, vendor behavior across contracts) matters more than presence
3. Industry/project complexity is a necessary control variable in any risk model

**Signal specificity:** The risk score should distinguish:
- High-complexity project + aditivo ≤ 25% = lower risk
- Simple project + aditivo near 25% = higher risk
- Any project + multiple consecutive aditivos = high risk (strategic pattern)
