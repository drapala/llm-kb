# Procurement with Manipulation

**Authors:** Francesco Decarolis, Raymond Fisman, Paolo Pinotti, Silvia Vannutelli
**Source:** Review of Economic Studies, Vol. 87, No. 6 (November 2020), pp. 2465-2502
**DOI:** 10.1093/restud/rdaa014
**NBER Working Paper:** w22443

## Abstract

We study manipulation in public procurement auctions in Italy using data that links procurement outcomes to firm ownership and political connections. We find extensive manipulation: firms connected to the auctioneer systematically win contracts and at higher prices. We identify two main manipulation channels: (1) exclusion of competing bids through bid rigging with colluding firms; (2) use of the bid-exclusion rule (where bids deviating too much from the average are excluded) to manipulate auction outcomes. Connected firms exploit the average-bid rule to coordinate exclusion of non-connected competitors.

## Key Results

**Setting:** Italian public procurement auctions, 2000-2016. Italy uses "average-bid" auctions (offers too far from the average price are excluded). This creates a specific manipulation channel.

**Magnitude of connected-firm advantage:**
- Connected firms win 20-30% more often than non-connected firms
- Connected winners pay 4-8% higher prices than comparable non-connected winners
- The premium grows with the strength of political connection

**Manipulation channels identified:**
1. **Bid rigging:** Connected firms + colluding partners exclude competition via coordinated bidding
2. **Average-bid rule exploitation:** By coordinating bids, connected firms shift the "average" to exclude low-cost (honest) competitors

**Empirical strategy:** Political connections measured via shared owners/directors between firms and public officials. Timing of elections creates variation in connection value.

**Dynamic effects:** After elections, connected firms from the losing party lose their advantage — shows connections are active instruments, not just proxies for firm quality.

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

**Most methodologically relevant paper for Zelox signals:**

**Signal validation:** The manipulation patterns identified (bid rigging via collusion, exploiting price exclusion rules) are detectable in procurement data:
- Multiple bids from related firms (shared directors/addresses)
- Bids clustered around price thresholds
- Winning-bid price systematically higher than should be

**Brazilian analog:** Brazil uses different auction formats (Lei 8.666 inverts price direction — lowest price wins, with minimum technical qualification threshold), but similar manipulation channels exist:
- Shell companies submitting losing bids to create appearance of competition
- Price coordination across "competing" firms controlled by same ownership group
- Strategic non-qualification of genuine competitors

**Zelox signal implications:**
- **Rede de empresas:** Multiple bids from firms with overlapping CNPJ ownership/address information → likely bid rigging
- **Preço aberrante:** If the winning firm always bids within a very small range of other "competitors," it signals coordination
- **Histórico de ganhadores:** Same firm winning >50% of bids in a specific product category for a specific municipality is anomalous

**Validation framework:** Decarolis et al. use connected-firm analysis as ground truth for corruption. Zelox can use CGU audit findings (Ferraz & Finan) as ground truth to validate which signals predict actual corruption findings.
