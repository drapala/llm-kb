# Electoral Accountability and Corruption: Evidence from the Audits of Local Governments

**Authors:** Claudio Ferraz and Frederico Finan
**Source:** American Economic Review, Vol. 101, No. 4 (June 2011), pp. 1274-1311
**URL:** https://eml.berkeley.edu/~ffinan/Finan_Termlimits.pdf

## Abstract

This paper exploits variation in Brazil's term limits to study the effect of reelection incentives on the incidence of corruption. Using audit data from Brazil's CGU federal program, we find that municipalities with a mayor in the last term in office exhibit significantly more corruption than those governed by a first-term mayor eligible for reelection. The effect is stronger in municipalities with more access to local media (radio). These results suggest that the threat of voter accountability provides an important disciplining mechanism.

## Key Results

**Term limit comparison:** Last-term mayors (not eligible for reelection) show significantly more corruption than first-term mayors facing reelection. Effect: corruption increases by ~0.35 violations (≈20% of mean) in last-term municipalities.

**Media amplification:** The accountability effect (first-term → less corruption) is stronger in municipalities with local radio. Without media, reelection incentives are weaker because information doesn't reach voters.

**Mechanism:** Electoral accountability works as a disciplining mechanism for corruption. When voters can sanction via the ballot box, politicians reduce corrupt behavior. When term limits eliminate this threat, corruption increases.

## Contrast with Ferraz & Finan (2008)

| Paper | Question | Variation used | Finding |
|-------|----------|---------------|---------|
| (2008) | Does *revealing* corruption punish incumbents? | Timing of audit release | Yes: -7 to -29pp reelection |
| (2011) | Does *threat* of revelation reduce corruption? | Term limits | Yes: last-term mayors ≈20% more corrupt |

Together, the two papers identify both sides of the accountability mechanism:
- (2008): voters punish when they learn → accountability works from the demand side
- (2011): politicians behave better when threatened → accountability works from the supply side

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

**When is corruption detectable?** The (2011) paper implies that corruption is *more likely* in:
- Last-term politicians (no reelection incentive)
- Municipalities without local media (less voter monitoring)
- Late in the electoral cycle (incentives weakest)

For procurement risk scoring: supplier corruption is more likely when the *buyer* (município) also has weak accountability — e.g., single-party dominance, low media penetration, last-term officials.

**Systemic risk indicator:** Municipalities with last-term mayors + no local radio are predicted to have higher corruption in procurement. This could be a cross-feature for Zelox's municipal-level risk layer (not just supplier-level).
