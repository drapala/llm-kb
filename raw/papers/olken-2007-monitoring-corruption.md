# Monitoring Corruption: Evidence from a Field Experiment in Indonesia

**Author:** Benjamin A. Olken
**Source:** Journal of Political Economy, Vol. 115, No. 2 (April 2007), pp. 200-249
**DOI:** 10.1086/511734
**NBER Working Paper:** w11753

## Abstract

This paper uses a randomized field experiment to study whether auditing reduces corruption in government programs. I study a road-building program in over 600 Indonesian villages. Villages were randomly assigned to varying levels of government audits or grass-roots monitoring by village meetings. I find that government audits substantially reduced missing expenditures by about 8 percentage points, but grass-roots monitoring had no significant effect. The finding that top-down auditing reduces corruption, while community monitoring may not, has important implications for anti-corruption policy.

## Key Results

**Experimental design:** 608 Indonesian villages building roads were randomly assigned to:
- Increased probability of government audit (from 4% to 100%)
- Grass-roots monitoring (village meetings with invited outside engineers)
- Control

**Corruption measure:** "Missing expenditures" = difference between reported expenditures and independent engineer estimate of actual project cost. Mean: ~24% of total project cost.

**Main findings:**
1. Government audits (increased probability): reduced missing expenditures by ~8 percentage points relative to mean of 24%. Effect is large and significant.
2. Grass-roots monitoring (village meetings): no significant effect on corruption.
3. The audit probability effect is nonlinear — a jump from near-zero to certain audit drives the reduction.

**Mechanism:** Anticipation of audit changes behavior *ex-ante*. Contractors who know they will be audited reduce theft. Effect is strongest for forms of corruption easiest to detect in audits (materials quantities) vs harder (labor prices).

## Why Auditing Works (and Community Monitoring Doesn't)

Government audits provide *objective technical assessment* — engineers can measure road thickness, material quality, quantities. Community members can observe some things (is the road there?) but cannot verify technical specifications without expertise.

This suggests:
- Technical complexity of procurement → community monitoring insufficient
- Specialized technical audits are necessary and effective
- The *certainty* of audit matters more than the level of scrutiny

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

**Fundamental validation:** This paper provides causal evidence that audit risk changes contractor behavior. A risk score that increases perceived audit probability — even without actual audits — should reduce fraudulent behavior by the Olken mechanism.

**Detection vs deterrence:** Olken identifies two uses of auditing:
1. *Detection:* finds corruption after the fact (Ferraz & Finan 2008)
2. *Deterrence:* changes behavior before corruption happens (Olken 2007)

A real-time risk score enables deterrence more than detection — it makes contracts *seem* like they have audit probability = 1 from the contractor's perspective.

**Signal calibration:** Olken shows missing expenditures are most detectable in easily measurable dimensions (materials quantities, road length). For Zelox, signals that are verifiable against technical specs (unit prices vs market rates, quantities vs scope) should be most reliable.
