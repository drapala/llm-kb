# Audit Risk and Rent Extraction: Evidence from Municipal Procurement in Brazil

**Authors:** Yves Zamboni and Stephan Litschig
**Source:** Journal of Development Economics, Vol. 134 (September 2018), pp. 133-149
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jdeveco.2018.04.006

## Abstract

We exploit the randomization of Brazil's anti-corruption audit program to estimate the effect of audit risk on rent extraction in municipal procurement. Municipalities assigned to higher audit probability (through the CGU program) show significantly lower procurement irregularities. Effects are concentrated in municipalities with weaker local accountability institutions. Findings suggest that top-down auditing and local accountability are substitutes, not complements.

## Key Results

**Identification:** Same CGU audit lottery as Ferraz & Finan (2008, 2011), but focus on *procurement irregularities* as outcome (vs electoral outcomes in F&F).

**Main finding:** Higher audit probability → significantly fewer procurement irregularities. The 2x increase in audit probability reduced irregular procurement by approximately 15-20%.

**Substitution effect:** Audit impact is strongest in municipalities with *weak* local accountability (few civil society organizations, low education, no local press). Where local accountability is strong, audits have less marginal impact.

**Implication:** Top-down auditing and bottom-up accountability are substitutes — both reduce corruption through different channels, but you need one or the other.

## Types of Procurement Irregularities Detected

Building on Ferraz & Finan's coding:
1. **No competitive bidding:** contract awarded without required bidding process
2. **Overpricing:** prices substantially above market reference
3. **Phantom firms:** payments to non-existent entities
4. **Bid rigging:** evidence of collusion among bidders

## Relevance for B2G / Risk Score

**Most direct validation paper for Zelox:** Zamboni & Litschig (2018) directly estimates the effect of increased audit risk on *procurement irregularities* (not just electoral outcomes). This is the closest empirical analog to what Zelox does — increasing perceived audit risk via real-time scoring.

**Magnitude:** ~15-20% reduction in irregularities per doubling of audit probability. If Zelox's risk score proxies for audit probability in the minds of procurement officials, expected impact is in this range.

**Substitution for local accountability:** In municipalities with weak oversight institutions (likely to be where corrupt procurement is concentrated), audit-based monitoring has largest impact. Zelox should be most valuable for: lower-tier municipalities, less competitive markets, weak institutional environments.

**Signal validation:** The irregularity types identified (no bidding, overpricing, phantom firms, bid rigging) directly correspond to detectable patterns in PNCP data — validating that these are real and measurable signals, not just theoretical constructs.
