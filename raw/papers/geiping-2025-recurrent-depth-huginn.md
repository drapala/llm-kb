Aside from using recurrence to move along sequences, as in recurrent neural networks, it was understood early to also be the key to adaptive computation\n(Schmidhuber,\n2012\n; Graves,\n2017\n)\n. For transformers, recurrence was applied in\nDehghani et\u00a0al. (\n2019\n)\n, who highlight the aim of recurrent depth to model\nuniversal\n, i.e. Turing-complete, machines\n(Graves et\u00a0al.,\n2014\n)\n. It was used at scale (but with fixed recurrence) in\nLan et\u00a0al. (\n2019\n)\nand an interesting recent improvement in this line of work are described in\nTan et\u00a0al. (\n2023\n); Abnar et\u00a0al. (\n2023\n)\n,\nMathur et\u00a0al. (\n2024\n)\nand\nCsord\u00e1s et\u00a0al. (\n2024\n)\n.\nSchwarzschild et\u00a0al. (\n2021b\n); Bansal et\u00a0al. (\n2022\n); Bear et\u00a0al. (\n2024\n)\nand\nMcLeish et\u00a0al. (\n2024\n)\nshow that depth recurrence is advantageous when learning generalizable algorithms when training with randomized unrolling and input injections. Recent work has described depth-recurrent,\nlooped\n, transformers and studied their potential benefits with careful theoretical and small-scale analysis\n(Giannou et\u00a0al.,\n2023\n; Gatmiry et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n; Yang et\u00a0al.,\n2024a\n; Fan et\u00a0al.,\n2025\n)\n.\nFrom another angle, these models can be described as neural networks learning a fixed-point iteration, as studied in\ndeep equilibrium\nmodels\n(Bai et\u00a0al.,\n2019\n;\n2022\n)\n. They are further related to diffusion models\n(Song and Ermon,\n2019\n)\n, especially latent diffusion models\n(Rombach et\u00a0al.,\n2022\n)\n, but we note that language diffusion models are usually run with a per-sequence, instead of a per-token, iteration count\n(Lee et\u00a0al.,\n2018\n)\n. A key difference of our approach to both equilibrium models and diffusion models is in the training objective, where equilibrium methods solve the \u201cdirect\u201d problem\n(Geiping and Moeller,\n2019\n)\n, diffusion models solve a surrogate training objective, and our work suggests that truncated unrolling is a scalable alternative.\nMore generally, all architectures that recur in depth can also be understood as directly learning the analog to the gradient of a latent energy-based model\n(LeCun and Huang,\n2005\n; LeCun,\n2022\n)\n, to an implicitly defined intermediate optimization layer\n(Amos and Kolter,\n2017\n)\n, or to a Kuramoto layer\n(Miyato et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n. Analogies to gradient descent at inference time also show the connection to test time adaptation\n(Sun et\u00a0al.,\n2020\n)\n, especially test-time adaptation of output states\n(Boudiaf et\u00a0al.,\n2022\n)\n.\nAside from full recurrent-depth architectures, there also exist a number of proposals for hybrid architectures, such as models with latent sub-networks\n(Li et\u00a0al.,\n2020a\n)\n, LoRA adapters on top of weight-shared layers\n(Bae et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n, or (dynamic) weight-tying of trained models\n(Hay and Wolf,\n2023\n; Liu et\u00a0al.,\n2024b\n)\n.\nAs mentioned in\nSection\n6\n, while we consider the proposed recurrent depth approach to be a very natural way to learn to reason in continuous latent space from the ground up, the works of\nHao et\u00a0al. (\n2024\n); Cheng and Durme (\n2024\n)\nand\nLiu et\u00a0al. (\n2024a\n)\ndiscuss how to finetune existing fixed-depth transformers with this capability. These works have a similar aim to ours, enabling reasoning in latent space, but approach this goal from separate directions.\nFor additional discussions related to the idea of constructing a prior that incentivizes reasoning and algorithm learning at the expense of memorization of simple patterns, we also refer to\nChollet (\n2019\n)\n,\nSchwarzschild (\n2023\n)\n,\nLi et\u00a0al. (\n2020b\n)\nand\nMoulton (\n2023\n)\n.\n9\nFuture Work\nAside from work extending and analyzing the scaling behaviors of recurrent depth models, there are many questions that remain unanswered. For example, to us, there are potentially a large number of novel post-training schemes that further enhance the capabilities of these models, such as fine-tuning to compress the recurrence or reinforcement learning with data with different hardness levels\n(Zelikman et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n, or to internalize reasoning from CoT data into the recurrence\n(Deng et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n.\nAnother aspect not covered in this work is the relationship to other modern architecture improvements. Efficient sequence mixing operations, especially those that are linear in sequence dimension, such as linear attention\n(Katharopoulos et\u00a0al.,\n2020\n; Yang et\u00a0al.,\n2024b\n)\n, are limited in the number of comparisons that can be made. However, with recurrent depth, blocks containing linear operators can repeat until all necessary comparisons between sequence elements are computed\n(Suzgun et\u00a0al.,\n2019\n)\n. For simplicity, we also focus on a single recurrence, where prior work has considered multiple successive recurrent stages\n(Takase and Kiyono,\n2023\n; Csord\u00e1s et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n.\nFinally, the proposed architecture is set up to be\ncompute-heavy\n, with more \u201cmaterialized\u201d parameters than there are actual parameters. This naturally mirrors mixture-of-expert models (MoE), which are\nparameter-heavy\n, using fewer active parameters per forward pass than exist within the model\n(Shazeer et\u00a0al.,\n2017\n; Fedus et\u00a0al.,\n2022\n)\n. We posit that where the recurrent-depth setup excels at learning reasoning patterns, the MoE excels at effectively storing and retrieving complex information. Their complementarity supports the hypothesis that a future architecture would contain both modifications. While in a standard MoE model, each expert can only be activated once per forward pass, or skipped entirely, a recurrent MoE model could also refine its latent state over multiple iterations, potentially routing to the same expert multiple times, before switching to a different one\n(Tan et\u00a0al.,\n2023\n; Csord\u00e1s et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n. While MoE models are the currently leading solution to implement this type of \u201cmemory\u201d in dense transformers, these considerations also hold for other memory mechanisms suggested for LLMs\n(Sukhbaatar et\u00a0al.,\n2019\n; Fan et\u00a0al.,\n2021\n; Wu et\u00a0al.,\n2022\n; He et\u00a0al.,\n2024\n)\n.\n10\nConclusions\nThe models described in this paper are ultimately still a proof-of-concept. We describe how to train a latent recurrent-depth architecture, what parameters we chose, and then trained a single model at scale. Future training runs are likely to train with more optimized learning rate schedules, data mixes and accelerators. Still we observe a number of interesting behaviors emerging naturally from recurrent training. The most important of these is the ability to use latent reasoning to dramatically improve performance on reasoning tasks by expending test-time computation. In addition, we also observe context-dependent convergence speed, path independence, and various zero-shot abilities.\nThis leads us to believe that latent reasoning is a promising research direction to complement existing approaches for test-time compute scaling. The model we realize is surprisingly powerful given its size and amount of training data, and we are excited about the potential impact of imbuing generative models with the ability to reason in continuous latent space without the need for specialized data at train time or verbalization at inference time.\nAcknowledgements\nThis project was made possible by the INCITE program: An award for computer time was provided by the U.S. Department of Energy\u2019s (DOE) Innovative and Novel Computational Impact on Theory and Experiment (INCITE) Program. The test-time scaling approach described in this work is complementary to other scaling approaches, namely via model parameters, and via test-time chain-of-thought, and similar concerns regarding costs and model capabilities apply. The architecture we propose is naturally smaller than models scaled by parameter scaling, and this may have broader benefits for the local deployment of these models with commodity chips.
